Diwali, the festival of lights is indeed the most awaited and the most celebrated festivals of India. People in every nook and cranny of the country welcome the festival with enthusiastic gestures. This wonderful festival is the celebration of five days. On the third day of the celebratory occasion, the key rituals of the Diwali festival take place. Lighting of Diyas and candles all around the house, worshipping the Laxmi Ganesha to summon health and wealth and bursting crackers are the chief rituals of the festival.

1. Dhanteras (Day of Prosperity)


Significance: The first day of Diwali, Dhanteras (or Dhanatrayodashi), marks the beginning of the festival. It is associated with wealth and prosperity.
Rituals: People clean and decorate their homes and offices to welcome prosperity. Traditionally, on this day, new utensils, jewelry, or other valuable items are purchased. Worship of Lord Dhanvantari (the god of medicine and health) and Lord Kubera (the god of wealth) takes place. Symbolism: Buying new items is believed to bring good luck and fortune for the upcoming year.

2. Naraka Chaturdashi (Choti Diwali)


Significance: Also known as Kali Chaudas or Choti Diwali, this day is observed to celebrate the victory of Lord Krishna over the demon Narakasura, symbolizing the victory of good over evil.
Rituals: People wake up early, take oil baths (abhyanga), and wear new clothes. Small, symbolic fireworks are lit. In some regions, it’s also believed to be a day to fend off negative energies. Symbolism: Naraka Chaturdashi represents the removal of darkness and the cleansing of one’s soul.


3. Lakshmi Puja
(Main Diwali)


Significance:The third and most significant day of Diwali is Lakshmi Puja. This day is dedicated to Goddess Lakshmi, the goddess of wealth, prosperity, and beauty.
Rituals: Homes are illuminated with lamps (diyas), candles, and electric lights. Rangolis are made at the entrance of houses to welcome the goddess. People perform Lakshmi Puja to invoke blessings for wealth and prosperity. The night is filled with the bursting of fireworks, family gatherings, and feasts. Symbolism: It signifies the triumph of light over darkness and knowledge over ignorance.

4. Govardhan Puja (Padwa or Annakut)


Significance: On the fourth day, Govardhan Puja, people celebrate Lord Krishna’s act of lifting Mount Govardhan to protect villagers from torrential rains sent by Lord Indra.
Rituals: In some regions, devotees prepare large quantities of food (Annakut) to offer to the deity. In North India, this day is also known as Padwa, and it celebrates the bond between husband and wife. In Maharashtra, newlyweds are invited to feast and are showered with gifts and blessings. Symbolism: The day represents gratitude for nature and a reminder to stay humble and connected to the earth.

5. Bhai Dooj (Brother-Sister Bond)


Significance: The final day of Diwali, Bhai Dooj (also known as Bhau Beej or Bhai Tika), celebrates the bond between brothers and sisters.
Rituals: Sisters apply a ceremonial tika (vermilion mark) on their brothers' foreheads, praying for their well-being and long life. Brothers, in return, give gifts and promise to protect their sisters.
Symbolism: It’s a day of familial bonding, strengthening the relationship between siblings. Each day of Diwali contributes to the overall theme of light conquering darkness, knowledge overcoming ignorance, and the importance of family, wealth, and well-being.
